# TTB COLA Process: Real-World Context for AI-Powered Label Verification

## 1. What Is a COLA?

A **Certificate of Label Approval (COLA)** is a federal authorization issued by the **Alcohol and Tobacco Tax and Trade Bureau (TTB)** — a division of the U.S. Department of the Treasury — that permits an alcoholic beverage to be bottled and sold in U.S. interstate or foreign commerce. No alcohol producer, importer, or bottler may release a product for sale without first obtaining a COLA (or a Certificate of Exemption) for each distinct label.

The COLA system exists to enforce the **Federal Alcohol Administration Act (FAA Act)**, which gives TTB authority to regulate the labeling, advertising, and sale of beverage alcohol to prevent consumer deception, protect public health, and ensure fair competition.

---

## 2. Regulatory Framework

TTB labeling rules are codified in **Title 27 of the Code of Federal Regulations (27 CFR)**. Each product category has its own part:

| Product Type       | CFR Part | Notes                                                        |
|--------------------|----------|--------------------------------------------------------------|
| Wine               | Part 4   | Applies to wine >7% ABV; covers appellation, vintage, sulfites |
| Distilled Spirits  | Part 5   | Whiskey, vodka, gin, rum, tequila, RTDs, etc.               |
| Malt Beverages     | Part 7   | Beer, ale, lager, FMBs (flavored malt beverages)            |
| Health Warning     | Part 16  | Universal; applies to all three categories                  |

These regulations govern what information **must** appear on a label (mandatory elements), what **may** appear (optional elements with conditions), and what is **prohibited** (false, misleading, or obscene claims).

---

## 3. Who Must Apply

COLA requirements apply to:
- **Domestic bottlers** of distilled spirits and wine (>7% ABV)
- **Importers** bottling in the U.S. or importing pre-bottled products
- **Malt beverage producers** whose products cross state lines

Exceptions exist for malt beverages sold exclusively within a single state — those producers are not required to obtain a COLA but may still need state-level label approvals.

Third-party filers (law firms, trade consultants) may submit on behalf of producers. This is common for large portfolios or international importers.

---

## 4. The COLA Application Process

### Submission

Applications are submitted electronically via **COLAs Online** (TTB's web portal) using **TTB Form 5100.31**. The submission requires:

- High-resolution images of **every label panel** (JPG or PNG only — TIFF and PDF are rejected)
- Product metadata: alcohol type, container size, ABV, class/type designation
- Bottler or importer name and address
- A unique TTB ID is assigned immediately upon submission

There is **no filing fee**. COLAs Online is available 22 hours/day, 7 days/week.

### Review

Each application is reviewed by a **TTB specialist** (human examiner) who checks compliance with the FAA Act and relevant CFR regulations. The process:

1. Application received → unique TTB ID assigned
2. Specialist reviews label images and metadata
3. Three possible outcomes:
   - **Approved**: COLA issued; product may be bottled
   - **Needs Correction**: Returned with itemized deficiency list; submitter has **30 calendar days** to revise
   - **Rejected**: Application denied; new submission required

### Timeline

- **Typical**: 5–15 business days
- **Average** (including corrections): ~24 days
- Errors, missing information, or high submission volume can extend this significantly

### COLA Registry

All approved, expired, surrendered, and revoked COLAs are publicly searchable via the **TTB Public COLA Registry** at ttbonline.gov. This is a valuable data source for competitive research, port of entry checks, and compliance audits.

## 5. Mandatory Label Elements by Product Type

### 5A. Distilled Spirits (27 CFR Part 5)

The following must appear in the **same field of vision** (i.e., on the same label panel):
- **Brand name**
- **Class/type designation** (e.g., "Straight Bourbon Whiskey," "Vodka," "Blended Scotch Whisky")
- **Alcohol content** expressed as % ABV

Additional mandatory elements (may appear anywhere on the label):
- **Bottler's or importer's name and address**
- **Net contents** (container volume)
- **Government health warning statement** (see Section 6)
- **Country of origin** (imports only)

Conditional mandatory elements:
- Age statement (required if under 4 years for certain whiskey classes)
- Coloring matter or flavoring disclosure
- FD&C Yellow No. 5 (tartrazine) if present
- Aspartame disclosure
- Carmine or cochineal extract disclosure
- Sulfite declaration
- State of distillation (for certain products)
- Commodity statement (neutral spirits)

### 5B. Wine (27 CFR Part 4)

Mandatory elements:
- **Brand name**
- **Class/type designation** (e.g., "Table Wine," "Champagne," "Port")
- **Alcohol content** (% ABV or range for certain table wines)
- **Bottler's name and address** (or importer's)
- **Net contents**
- **Government health warning statement**
- **Sulfite declaration**: "Contains sulfites" required if SO₂ detected at ≥10 ppm
- **Country of origin** (imports)

Optional but regulated elements:
- **Appellation of origin**: If used, ≥75% of fruit must come from that region (≥85% for most state and county AVA designations)
- **Vintage date**: At least 95% of wine must be from that harvest year; requires appellation
- **Varietal designation**: ≥75% of named grape variety; requires appellation

### 5C. Malt Beverages / Beer (27 CFR Part 7)

Mandatory elements:
- **Brand name**
- **Name and address** of the brewer or importer
- **Net contents**
- **Government health warning statement**
- **Country of origin** (imports)

Conditionally mandatory:
- **Alcohol content**: Required only when ABV derives from flavors or added non-beverage ingredients (not simply for high-ABV beer)
- **FD&C Yellow No. 5** disclosure
- **Sulfite declaration** if present
- **Aspartame disclosure** if present
- **Cochineal extract or carmine** disclosure

Note: Malt beverages without interstate commerce do not require federal COLA approval (state laws still apply).

## 6. Government Health Warning Statement

Mandated by the **Alcoholic Beverage Labeling Act of 1988 (ABLA)**, applicable to **all** products with ≥0.5% ABV.

**Required verbatim text:**
> GOVERNMENT WARNING: (1) According to the Surgeon General, women should not drink alcoholic beverages during pregnancy because of the risk of birth defects. (2) Consumption of alcoholic beverages impairs your ability to drive a car or operate machinery, and may cause health problems.

**Formatting requirements:**
- "GOVERNMENT WARNING" must appear in **bold capital letters**
- Minimum type size is container-volume-dependent (measured in mm, not points)
- Must appear on a **contrasting background**
- Must be **clearly separated** from other label information
- Must be **readily legible under ordinary conditions**

**Penalties:** Civil penalties up to **$26,225 per day** of non-compliance as of 2025.

---

## 7. Prohibited Label Content

TTB prohibits the following categories of label content:

| Category | Examples |
|----------|---------|
| **False/misleading statements** | Misrepresenting age, origin, identity, or composition |
| **Obscene/indecent material** | Images or language violating decency standards |
| **Disparagement** | False statements attacking competitors' products |
| **Prohibited health claims** | Unsubstantiated therapeutic or curative claims; statements suggesting alcohol has health benefits |
| **Geographic misdirection** | Implying geographic origin that isn't substantiated (e.g., "Kentucky Bourbon" for a non-KY product) |
| **"Pure" designation** | Use of the word "pure" on distilled spirits labels |
| **Misleading nutrient claims** | References to carbohydrates, calories, or macronutrients in misleading ways |

Health-related statements ARE permitted if they are: truthful, scientifically substantiated, properly qualified, and include disclosures about health risks of alcohol consumption — but TTB scrutinizes these closely and the bar is high.

---

## 8. Common Rejection Reasons

Understanding why labels fail is directly relevant to building an AI verification tool. The most frequent rejection triggers:

1. **Government warning errors**: Wrong text, missing comma, insufficient font size, inadequate contrast, not separated from other elements
2. **Incorrect class/type designation**: Especially problematic for craft products, RTDs, infused spirits, and novel formulations
3. **Missing mandatory elements**: Net contents, name/address, ABV in wrong position
4. **Image file format**: TTB only accepts JPEG or PNG — submitting TIFF or PDF causes automatic rejection
5. **Field-of-vision violations**: Brand name, class/type, and ABV not co-located on the same panel (distilled spirits)
6. **Unsubstantiated geographic claims**: Using a regional name without meeting the minimum fruit sourcing percentage
7. **Misleading statements**: Age implications, quality claims, health suggestions
8. **Sulfite declaration omissions**: Missing "contains sulfites" on wine with ≥10 ppm SO₂
9. **Country of origin missing**: Required on all imported products

## 9. Proposed and Recent Rule Changes

### January 2025 NPRMs (Notices of Proposed Rulemaking)

TTB published two significant NPRMs proposing major expansions of mandatory label disclosures:

**NPRM 1 — Alcohol Facts Statement:**
- Modeled on FDA nutrition labels
- Would require: serving size, calories, alcohol content per serving, protein, carbohydrates
- Proposed compliance date: 5 years after final rule publication

**NPRM 2 — Major Food Allergen Disclosure:**
- Would require disclosure of major allergens used in production: milk, eggs, fish, shellfish, tree nuts, wheat, peanuts, soybeans, sesame
- Same 5-year compliance runway proposed

**Status (as of mid-2025):** Still in rulemaking. Ingredient labeling rules remain under review at the White House Office of Management and Budget. These proposals represent the **most significant expansion of mandatory label content since the ABLA in 1988**.

### Why This Matters for AI Verification

Any AI-powered COLA verification system must be built for **regulatory change**. The proposed Alcohol Facts and allergen rules will add new mandatory fields requiring optical detection and validation. Systems that hard-code the current rule set will need structural updates as final rules are published.

## 10. Industry Scale and Economic Context

### Market Size

- **~9,000+ active wineries** in the U.S. (as of 2024)
- **~9,500+ craft breweries** operating
- **~2,500+ craft distilleries**
- **Tens of thousands of importers** filing COLAs annually
- The TTB Public COLA Registry contains **millions of records** — the volume of active SKUs in the market is enormous

### Compliance Cost

- A rejected or delayed COLA can halt a production run, delay market entry, and cost thousands in lost revenue per day
- Regulatory attorneys and compliance consultants charge $200–$600/hour for label review
- Large producers employ in-house regulatory affairs teams dedicated to COLA management
- Small/craft producers are most vulnerable: they lack compliance staff and often learn requirements by trial and error

### Current Tooling Landscape

Existing compliance tools fall into two categories:

**Workflow/submission tools:**
- Sovos ShipCompliant: Automates state-level product and label registrations across 15+ government systems; used by 2,000+ beverage alcohol companies
- COLA Cloud, Signify, and similar platforms: Help manage the COLA application pipeline

**Quality/defect detection (manufacturing):**
- Vision systems on bottling lines for fill heights, cap torque, label placement
- These detect physical defects, not regulatory compliance issues

**Gap:** No widely-adopted AI tool currently performs pre-submission regulatory compliance checking of label images against TTB's mandatory element requirements. The closest are manual checklists provided by TTB itself (the "Anatomy of a Label" and "Checklist of Mandatory Label Information" resources on ttb.gov).

## 11. AI Opportunity Analysis

### What AI Can Verify Automatically

| Check | Feasibility |
|-------|------------|
| Presence/absence of required text elements | High — OCR + NLP |
| Government warning text accuracy (verbatim match) | High — text extraction + string comparison |
| Font size compliance | Medium — requires calibration to container dimensions |
| Contrast ratio of warning statement | High — image analysis |
| Field-of-vision co-location (distilled spirits) | High — spatial layout detection |
| Class/type designation format | Medium — NLP pattern matching against TTB-defined list |
| ABV format and range plausibility | High — regex + domain rules |
| Sulfite declaration presence (wines) | High — text detection |
| Country of origin (imports) | High — entity extraction |
| Prohibited terms detection | Medium-High — keyword lists + NLP |

### What Remains Difficult for AI

- **Substantiation checks**: Is the geographic designation earned? Requires cross-referencing production records
- **Truthfulness of factual claims**: Did the product actually age X years? Requires supply chain data
- **Novel product classification**: New RTD formats, infused spirits, or non-traditional ingredients often require TTB guidance to classify correctly
- **Context-dependent prohibitions**: Whether a statement is "misleading" often requires legal judgment

### Design Implications

An effective AI verification prototype should:
1. Accept high-resolution label image(s) as input
2. Run OCR to extract all text from all label panels
3. Detect and verify mandatory elements per product type (wine/beer/spirits)
4. Flag missing, incorrect, or potentially prohibited content
5. Produce a structured compliance report with pass/fail/warning states per rule
6. Link each finding to the specific CFR section it relates to
7. Be product-type aware (different rule sets apply to wine vs. beer vs. spirits)
